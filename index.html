Part 1 · The Four Contingencies (highest yield)

Every contingency question is two questions in disguise:

  1. Was something ADDED or REMOVED after the behavior? Added = Positive (+). Removed = Negative (−). (Positive/negative are math signs, NOT good/bad.)
  2. Does the behavior INCREASE or DECREASE in the future? Increases = Reinforcement. Decreases = Punishment.
Behavior INCREASESBehavior DECREASES
Stimulus ADDED (+)Positive Reinforcement — kid cleans room → gets praise → cleans morePositive Punishment — kid touches stove → gets burned → stops touching
Stimulus REMOVED (−)Negative Reinforcement — you buckle seatbelt → beeping stops → you always buckleNegative Punishment — kid hits sibling → tablet taken away → hitting decreases

Parent-life anchors

  • Positive reinforcement: Toddler says "please," gets the cookie → says "please" more.
  • Negative reinforcement: Baby cries, you pick baby up, crying stops → you pick baby up faster next time (the removal of crying reinforced YOUR behavior). Also: ibuprofen removes a headache → you take it again.
  • Positive punishment: Child runs into street → you deliver a loud reprimand → running into street decreases.
  • Negative punishment: Teen misses curfew → loses car keys for a week → curfews stop being missed.
Two classic traps:
• Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment. It's an escape hatch — behavior goes UP because something aversive went away.
• It's only reinforcement/punishment if future behavior actually changes. The consequence's effect defines it, not the parent's intent.

Conditioned vs. Unconditioned consequences

  • Unconditioned (primary): works with no learning history — tied to survival. Food, water, warmth, sleep, escape from pain. (Cookie = unconditioned reinforcer; a burn = unconditioned punisher.)
  • Conditioned (secondary): got its power by being paired with other reinforcers/punishers. Praise, money, tokens, grades, stickers; reprimands, fines, "the look."
  • Hook: Unconditioned = Unlearned. If a newborn would respond to it, it's unconditioned.

Full label example: sticker chart → sticker after potty use → potty use increases = positive reinforcement with a conditioned reinforcer.

Part 2 · Theorists & the "-isms"

The three theorists — one line each

TheoristKnown forLettersBehaviorism
PavlovRespondent (classical) conditioning — dogs, bells, salivationS-R(physiologist, pre-behaviorism)
WatsonFounded behaviorism; S-R psychology; Little AlbertS-RMethodological
SkinnerOperant conditioning; EAB; Behavior of Organisms (1938); Skinner boxA-B-CRadical

Hook: Pavlov = Physiology & Pairing. Watson = Watchable-only. Skinner = Self-included (private events count).

Assumptions good science accepts

  • Determinism — the universe is lawful; behavior has causes we can discover. (Opposite of free will, fatalism, accidentalism.)
  • Pragmatism — truth is judged by what works; an intervention is "good" if it produces effective change.
  • Selectionism — behavior is selected by its consequences. Three levels: species (phylogeny), individual learning (ontogeny), culture.

Explanatory dead ends (spot the WRONG answer)

  • Mentalism — explains behavior by an inner dimension: mind, willpower, "he hit because he's angry inside." Relies on hypothetical constructs and explanatory fictions. ← answer to "which uses explanatory fiction?"
  • Fatalism — everything is predetermined and nothing you do can change it. ("He'll always be aggressive; it's just who he is.")
  • Accidentalism — behavior happens randomly, with no lawful causes. (Anti-determinism.)
The two behaviorisms (guaranteed question):
• Methodological (Watson): study ONLY publicly observable behavior. Private events off-limits. ← "does NOT include private events."
• Radical (Skinner): private events ARE behavior, same principles — just observable to one person. ← "DOES include private events." ("Radical" = thorough, not extreme.)

The three branches of behavior analysis

  1. Behaviorism — the philosophy of the science of behavior.
  2. Experimental Analysis of Behavior (EAB) — basic lab research discovering principles (Skinner, rats/pigeons, schedules). Founded with Behavior of Organisms (1938).
  3. Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) — applied science on socially significant behavior. Defined by Baer, Wolf & Risley (1968).
ABA — memorize: the science in which tactics derived from the principles of behavior are applied systematically to improve socially significant behavior, and experimentation is used to identify the variables responsible for behavior change.
  • "Skinner box with animals" → EAB (basic research).
  • "Teaching a child to request instead of tantrum" → ABA.

Part 3 · The 7 Dimensions of ABA

Established by Baer, Wolf, and Risley, 1968 — expect a direct question on this. Mnemonic: GET A CAB.

AnalyticDid we prove the intervention caused the change?Improves when reinforcement is on; reverses when off

Part 4 · Assumptions & Attitudes of Science

Mnemonic: Dr. PEEP² — Determinism, Replication, Parsimony, Empiricism, Experimentation, Philosophical Doubt. (6 attitudes, 2 P's, 2 E's.)

AssumptionMeaningScenario keyword
DeterminismBehavior is lawful — it has causes; nothing is random"assumes there's a reason," "not random"
EmpiricismObjective observation & measurement — data over opinion"directly observes," "takes data," "reviews the graph"
ExperimentationManipulate a variable and watch the effect"functional analysis," "reversal design," "introduces/withdraws"
ReplicationRepeat the experiment — same results build confidence"another team/classroom, same results"
ParsimonyRule out the simplest explanation first"before assuming trauma, checks reinforcement first"
Philosophical DoubtContinually question what we know; stay open"reviews new research," "willing to adjust," "keeps questioning"
Determinism vs. Free Will do NOT align. Science requires determinism — if behavior were uncaused, there'd be nothing to study.

Common confusion pairs

  • Empiricism vs. Experimentation: measuring/observing vs. changing something to see the effect. Watching = empiricism; manipulating = experimentation.
  • Replication vs. Experimentation: replication = doing the same study again (new people/places, same result).
  • Parsimony vs. Philosophical Doubt: simplest explanation first vs. healthy skepticism about all conclusions, always.

Part 5 · Key Terms

The building blocks

  • Behavior — anything a living organism does. Dead Man's Test: if a dead man can do it (sit still, "not hit"), it is NOT behavior — behavior must be an action, not the absence of one.
  • Environment — everything in the physical world except the behavior itself (internal body events count as environment for other behavior).
  • Stimulus — an energy change affecting the organism through receptor cells (sound, light, touch, smell).
  • Response — one single occurrence of behavior. (Behavior = the class; response = one instance.)
  • Response topography — the physical form/shape of a response (waving left vs. right hand = different topographies).
  • Antecedent — a stimulus change that occurs before behavior.
  • Consequence — a stimulus change that follows behavior.
  • Contingency — the "if-then" dependency: IF the behavior occurs, THEN the consequence follows.

Classes (favorite trick question)

  • Response class — a group of responses that serve the same function, even with different topographies (tap, call "Mom!", tug sleeve → all get attention).
  • Stimulus class — a group of stimuli sharing common elements: formal (look alike), temporal (same time), or functional (same effect).
  • The difference: response class = grouped behaviors (by what they DO); stimulus class = grouped events (by shared features/effects).

Operant vs. Respondent (the verb trick)

RespondentOperant
Caused byAntecedent stimulus (BEFORE)Consequences (what came AFTER in the past)
VerbELICITED (pulled out)EMITTED (put out), selected, shaped
ModelS-RA-B-C
Learned?Reflexive — born with itLearned through consequences
TheoristPavlov (& Watson)Skinner
ExamplesPupil constricts; salivating; startleTalking, cooking, driving, tantrums

Hook: SORE — S-R = respondent (reflex); Operant = Emitted & A-B-C. Also: Respondent = Reflex; Operant = Outcome-driven.

  • Reflex — a simple unlearned S-R relation (US→UR); respondent behavior. We have them for survival (eye blink, hand from heat, infant rooting).
  • Habituation — a decrease in responsiveness when the same stimulus repeats (you stop noticing the ticking clock).
  • Respondent conditioning (Pavlov) — pair NS + US until NS becomes a CS that elicits a CR. (Bell + food → bell alone → salivation.)
  • Respondent extinction — present the CS without the US → the CR fades. (Bell, never food → salivation stops.)
  • Operant conditioning (Skinner) — consequences change the future frequency of behavior.
  • Three-term contingency — A-B-C: the basic unit of analysis for operant behavior.
  • Behavior repertoire — all behaviors a person can do (or the subset for a task: "her math repertoire").

The antecedent team

  • Discriminative stimulus (SD) — an antecedent in whose presence behavior has been reinforced → more likely now (Grandma always says yes).
  • Stimulus delta (SΔ) — an antecedent in whose presence behavior has NOT been reinforced → less likely (Dad never says yes).
  • Discriminated operant — an operant that occurs more under some antecedents than others (behavior "under stimulus control").
  • Motivating operation (MO) — an event that (1) alters the value of a consequence and (2) alters the current frequency of related behavior. Food deprivation makes food more valuable AND food-seeking more likely now. (SD signals availability; MO changes how much you want it.)

Science & research terms

  • Science — a systematic approach to knowledge about the natural world, built on description, prediction, and control.
  • Philosophy — the study of fundamental assumptions; behaviorism is the philosophy underlying the science of behavior.
  • Independent variable (IV) — what the experimenter manipulates (the intervention). Hook: I change it.
  • Dependent variable (DV) — what is measured; in ABA, always the target behavior. It depends on the IV.

Three levels of scientific understanding

  1. Description — collecting observable facts → lets you organize/graph what's happening.
  2. Prediction — repeated observation shows two events correlate; use the past to assume the future. (Correlation, not causation.)
  3. Control — the highest level: manipulating one event reliably changes the other = a FUNCTIONAL RELATION. This is what the Analytic dimension demands.

Part 1 · Contingencies & Consequences (highest yield)

Same two-question trick as Exam 1: (1) Was something ADDED (+) or REMOVED (−)? (2) Did behavior INCREASE (reinforcement) or DECREASE (punishment)?

Behavior INCREASESBehavior DECREASES
Stimulus ADDED (+)Positive Reinforcement — hand up → teacher calls on her → hand-raising risesPositive Punishment — throws food → must wipe the whole table → throwing drops
Stimulus REMOVED (−)Negative Reinforcement — buckle seatbelt → chime stops → always bucklesNegative Punishment — curses → loses 15 min of phone (response cost) → cursing drops
Two classic traps (they LOVE these):
• Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment, and NOT "artificial." "Negative" only means a stimulus was removed; behavior still goes UP.
• Punishment is defined by its suppressive EFFECT on future behavior — not intent. No decrease = no punishment.

Escape vs. Avoidance (both are negative reinforcement — behavior goes UP)

  • Escape — the aversive thing is already here, and the response ENDS it. (Alarm blaring → cover ears → noise drops.)
  • Avoidance — the response happens first and PREVENTS the aversive thing from ever showing up. (Finish early → the warning never comes.)
  • Hook: Escape = Ends what's present · Avoidance = Averts what hasn't arrived.

The "nagging" question (two contingencies at once)

  • The child's cleaning is negatively reinforced — it turns off the aversive nagging.
  • The parent's nagging is also negatively reinforced — it "worked," so the parent nags more. Nagging functions as the aversive stimulus / EO.
"Do we reinforce people?" — No. We reinforce behavior. Saying "I reinforced Marcus" is technically wrong — you strengthened a response he made.

Conditioned vs. Unconditioned vs. Generalized

  • Unconditioned (primary): no learning history (phylogeny). Reinforcers: food, water, warmth, sleep, oxygen. Punishers: pain, extreme heat/cold, intense stimuli.
  • Conditioned (secondary): power through a history of pairing (ontogeny). Reinforcers: praise, grades, a token. Punishers: "No," a reprimand, a fine, "the look."
  • Generalized conditioned reinforcer: paired with many backups → works regardless of any single MO/deprivation state. Money and tokens are the classic examples.
  • Hook: Unconditioned = Unlearned · Generalized = "goes with everything."

Automatic vs. Socially mediated reinforcement

  • Automatic: the behavior produces its own consequence — no other person (humming alone, scratching an itch).
  • Socially mediated: another person delivers the consequence.
  • Tell: "even when completely alone / no one around" → automatic.

Classifications of reinforcers (for assessments): Edible · Tangible/leisure · Activity · Social (attention) · Automatic/sensory.

Part 2 · Antecedents & Motivation

TermWhat it does
Discriminative stimulus (SD)Response has been reinforced in its presence → signals reinforcement is AVAILABLE → behavior more likely (vending light ON)
Stimulus delta (SΔ)Response has NOT been reinforced → signals reinforcement unavailable → behavior less likely (vending light OFF)
Discriminated operantAn operant that occurs more in the presence of the SD — behavior "under stimulus control"
Motivating operation (MO)(1) alters the VALUE of a consequence and (2) alters the current frequency of behavior that produced it
The single most-tested distinction — SD vs. MO: the MO changes how much you WANT it (value-altering); the SD signals it's AVAILABLE. Deprivation is an MO; the vending light is an SD.

The two MOs

  • Establishing operation (EO): INCREASES value → evokes behavior. Deprivation (no food all day → food worth more). Hook: EO Elevates.
  • Abolishing operation (AO): DECREASES value → abates behavior. Satiation (just ate → food worth less). Hook: AO Abolishes.

The four-term contingency

MO → SD → R → C. Exam 2 adds the MO in front of the old A-B-C. Hungry (MO) → sees machine (SD) → inserts money (R) → gets snack, added (C).

Premack principle

Make a high-probability behavior contingent on a low-probability behavior to increase the low one. "First veggies (low-p), then dessert (high-p)." Order matters: work first, then play.

Rule-governed vs. Contingency-shaped

  • Rule-governed: controlled by a verbal statement of the contingency, before contacting consequences (new employee wears a hard hat because the sign says so).
  • Contingency-shaped: shaped by direct, repeated contact with the actual consequences.
Delayed consequences: immediate beats delayed. Delay weakens a consequence and blurs which response is being strengthened.

Part 3 · The Matching Law

Under concurrent schedules (two options, each with its own reinforcement), the relative rate of a response matches the relative rate of reinforcement for it. Behavior distributes in proportion to available reinforcement.

The graph: y = relative rate of responding, x = relative rate of reinforcement. Points fall on a straight diagonal line, slope ≈ 1.0 = a perfect positive (linear) correlation.

Application: to shift behavior toward a desired response, raise its relative rate of reinforcement — you don't have to punish the competing behavior.

Part 4 · Schedules of Reinforcement (the dense one)

The three big-picture schedules

  • Continuous (CRF = FR-1): reinforce every response. Best for acquisition.
  • Intermittent (INT): reinforce some responses. Best for maintenance + resistance to extinction. Use after the skill is learned.
  • Extinction (EXT): reinforce none → behavior decreases (often after an extinction burst).

The four simple schedules

Two questions: Ratio (# of RESPONSES) or Interval (TIME elapses, then first response)? Fixed (same each time) or Variable (an average)?

ScheduleReinforcement after…Response pattern
FR (fixed ratio)a set # of responsesHigh rate, then a post-reinforcement pause
VR (variable ratio)a varying # (avg.)Highest, steadiest rate; most extinction-resistant (gambling)
FI (fixed interval)first response after a set timeThe SCALLOP — pause, then accelerating
VI (variable interval)first response after a varying timeSteady, moderate (checking email)
Fast tells: depends on TIME? → Interval (FI, VI). Depends on # of RESPONSES? → Ratio (FR, VR). Scallops? → FI. Highest, most extinction-resistant? → VR.

Compound schedules — CM-cued / TM-muted

ScheduleSDs?Structure
ChainedYesComponents in sequence; only the last yields the terminal reinforcer; each step has its own SD
TandemNoSame as chained but no SDs marking the steps
MultipleYesComponents alternate; each gets its own reinforcement; each signaled by its own SD
MixedNoSame as multiple but no SDs
Concurrent(choice)Two+ schedules available at once → the arrangement behind the matching law
CM-cued = Chained & Multiple have SDs (discriminated). TM-muted = Tandem & Mixed have none.
Chained vs. Multiple: chain = one payoff at the end of a required sequence; multiple = each component pays off, alternating.

Part 5 · Preference & Reinforcer Assessments

Preference = what a person likes / will pick (identifies likely reinforcers). Reinforcer assessment = tests whether that item actually INCREASES behavior. "What do they pick?" vs. "Does it actually work?"

The 1-2-Many-Free family

AssessmentHow it's run
Single-stimulus (SS)ONE item at a time; record approach. Good for learners who can't compare; poor at ranking
Paired-stimulus (PS)TWO at a time (forced choice); cycle all pairs → clear preference hierarchy
MSWOArray of several; pick one, REMOVE it, re-present the rest → efficient ranking; array shrinks
Free-operantItems freely available; record duration; nothing removed/blocked → naturalistic, low risk
Contrived free-operantFree-operant, but the clinician deliberately arranges the items ("contrived" = arranged on purpose)
Brief free-operantA short-duration free-operant observation

MSWO = the chosen item is NOT put back ("without replacement"). "Contrived" = clinician set up the items on purpose; "naturalistic" = observe with whatever's already there.

Match the intervention to the FUNCTION — this is the heart of the applied questions.

Time-out = time-out from positive reinforcement. It only works if "time-in" is rich with reinforcement, it's kept brief, and no attention is given during it. Never use it for escape-maintained behavior — you'd be reinforcing escape.

Part 1 · Motivating Operations (biggest new topic)

The two effects EVERY MO has

  • Value-altering — changes how effective a consequence is right now: EO increases value (deprivation) · AO decreases value (satiation). Hooks: EO Elevates · AO Abolishes.
  • Behavior-altering — changes the current frequency of behavior that gets it: evocative = increases now (goes with EOs) · abative = decreases now (goes with AOs).
Don't mix the two axes: value-altering = establishing/abolishing; behavior-altering = evocative/abative. An EO evokes; an AO abates.
Deprivation increases reinforcer effectiveness (EO). Satiation decreases it (AO).

UMO vs. CMO

  • Unconditioned MO (UMO): value-altering effect is unlearned / biological — food, water, sleep, oxygen, activity deprivation; painful stimulation; increased temperature; sexual deprivation.
  • Conditioned MO (CMO): value-altering effect depends on a learning history. Three types: surrogate, reflexive, transitive.

The three CMOs — the table to memorize

CMOWhat it doesExampleKey tell
Surrogate (CMO-S)A neutral stimulus paired with a UMO/other MO takes on that MO's effectsA room always linked with food → entering evokes food-seeking even when not hungryPAIRING; it substitutes for the MO
Reflexive (CMO-R)Signals worsening conditions; its own onset raises the value of its own removalYellow cardstock predicts a hated task → seeing it evokes tantrum/escapeA warning; the one you'd eliminate; works on its own removal
Transitive (CMO-T)Establishes another stimulus as a reinforcer and evokes behavior that got itSealed M&M bag → makes "help/opening" valuable → evokes the mand "open!"Makes something else a reinforcer
The CMO discrimination questions:
• Which relates to pairing? → Surrogate.
• Which would you want eliminated, and which focuses on eliminating itself? → Reflexive.
• Which makes something else a reinforcer? → Transitive.
• Read the stem: "warning + bad event together" = reflexive; "neutral stimulus paired with an existing MO" = surrogate.

MO vs. SD (they love this)

MO changes how much you want the consequence (value). SD signals the consequence is available. Deprivation, the yellow cardstock, the sealed bag = MOs. The vending light = SD.

Part 2 · Stimulus Control, Generalization & Concepts

  • SD: the response has been reinforced in its presence → reinforcement available. SΔ: it has not → unavailable.
  • How do you know behavior is under stimulus control? It occurs more often given the SD than the SΔ — differential responding.
  • SΔ nuance: choosing the SΔ can mean no reinforcement or a lesser amount/quality/delay — it doesn't always mean zero.
  • Conditional discrimination: the correct response depends on more than one stimulus — classic form is matching-to-sample (the sample tells you which comparison is correct).
  • Generalization: responding similarly to stimuli that share features with the SD (more similarity → more generalization). Discrimination: responding differently to SD vs. SΔ. Opposite ends of one continuum.
Concept formation — be specific. A concept requires BOTH: (1) generalization WITHIN a stimulus class (respond to all examples) AND (2) discrimination BETWEEN classes (don't respond to non-examples). One alone isn't a concept.

Feature vs. Arbitrary stimulus classes

  • Feature class: members share common physical features — all triangles, all red things. If they look alike, it's a feature class.
  • Arbitrary class: no physical similarity; grouped by function / learning history — {apple, milk, bread, chicken} = "food".

Stimulus equivalence (Sidman) & salience

  • Reflexivity — A=A (identity matching, untrained). Symmetry — if A→B then B→A. Transitivity — if A→B and B→C then A→C.
  • The critical test: emergence of untrained relations — the combined symmetry-and-transitivity test: train A→B and B→C, then test C→A (reflexivity assumed).
  • Salience: how noticeable a stimulus is; more salient features gain stimulus control faster (and can cause overselectivity).

Faulty stimulus control & overselectivity

  • Faulty stimulus control: control by an irrelevant feature (answer position, an incidental color) — happens when an irrelevant stimulus is accidentally correlated with reinforcement. Fix: vary the irrelevant features, randomize position, use multiple exemplars, fade deliberately.
  • Stimulus overselectivity: control by only one feature (or a narrow subset) of a complex stimulus — common in autism.
  • Overselectivity vs. blocking: overselectivity = narrow attention within a compound; blocking = a previously learned cue prevents a newly added cue from gaining control.

Part 3 · Prompts & Transfer of Stimulus Control

The big split: Response prompts act on the response (verbal, gestural, model, physical) — help the behavior. Stimulus prompts act on the antecedent / SD itself (movement, position, redundancy/color) — change the stimulus.

Fading strategies

StrategyWhat it doesBest for
Most-to-Least (MTL)Start with the most intrusive prompt, fade to less over trialsNew / errorless acquisition
Least-to-Most (LTM)Start with the least help; add more only as neededLearner may already have the skill
Graduated guidancePhysical help adjusted moment-to-moment within the response (often shadowing)Real-time physical assistance
Time delaySame prompt, insert a time gap after the SD (constant or progressive)Transferring control by waiting
Time delay vs. MTL: time delay manipulates TIME (same prompt, delayed); MTL manipulates the PROMPT LEVEL.
MTL vs. graduated guidance: MTL fades on a preset plan across trials; graduated guidance adjusts in real time within each response.

Stimulus fading vs. stimulus shape transformation (asked twice — nail it)

  • Stimulus fading: gradually change a stimulus dimension (color, intensity, size) while the shape stays the same — a bold red letter fading to plain black.
  • Stimulus shape transformation: gradually change the actual SHAPE into the target form — an outline of a dog morphing step-by-step into the printed word "dog".
  • The difference: fading = change a feature/dimension, shape stays · shape transformation = change the shape itself.
  • Errorless learning: using prompts (MTL, time delay, fading) to prevent errors during acquisition, so wrong responses aren't practiced.

Part 4 · Measurement & IOA (big new topic)

Dimensional quantities of behavior

  • Repeatability: count (a tally) · rate / frequency = count per unit of time · celeration = change in rate over time.
  • Temporal extent: duration — how long the behavior lasts.
  • Temporal locus: response latency = SD onset → start of the response · interresponse time (IRT) = time between consecutive responses.
  • Derived: percentage, trials-to-criterion.
  • Which dimensions can be SHAPED? Frequency, latency, duration, magnitude/intensity — all of them.

Continuous vs. interval (discontinuous) measurement

MethodScore the interval if…BiasBest for
Whole-intervalbehavior occurs the ENTIRE intervalUNDER-estimatesbehaviors you want to increase (on-task)
Partial-intervalbehavior occurs at ANY pointOVER-estimatesbehaviors you want to decrease
Momentary time samplingbehavior occurs at the exact moment the interval endseither way; least biased when shortgroups / long observations
Interval lengths (know these): whole- and partial-interval must be short — generally ≤ ~15 seconds. Momentary time sampling: under ~2 minutes is highly concordant with continuous measurement; longer intervals lose accuracy.

Permanent product & measurement errors

  • Permanent product: measure behavior by its lasting outcome (completed worksheets, dishes washed). Favorable because you can measure after the fact — no live observation, less intrusive, saves time, and products can be re-scored.
  • Observer drift — the observer's definition gradually shifts. Reactivity — being observed changes the behavior. Observer bias — expectations color what's recorded.
IOA vs. treatment integrity: IOA = agreement between two observers on the DEPENDENT variable (the target behavior). Agreement on the INDEPENDENT variable (was the intervention delivered as designed?) is treatment integrity / procedural fidelity — not IOA. Assess both.

Part 5 · Schedules & Core-Principle Refresher

Limited hold (new term): a deadline for meeting a schedule's response requirement — once reinforcement becomes available, the response must occur within a set time window or that reinforcer is lost until the next opportunity. Ex: a VI 5-min schedule with a 30-second limited hold. Don't confuse it with the post-reinforcement pause.
  • Ratio = # of responses; Interval = time. Fixed = set; Variable = averaged. VR gambles (highest, most extinction-resistant); FI scallops. CRF → acquisition; intermittent → maintenance + extinction-resistance.
  • Compound: CM-cued (Chained & Multiple have SDs) vs. TM-muted (Tandem & Mixed have none); concurrent = two choices at once (the basis of the matching law).
Adding a stimulusRemoving a stimulus
Increase behaviorPositive ReinforcementNegative Reinforcement
Decrease behaviorPositive PunishmentNegative Punishment
  • Unconditioned = works with no learning (food, water / pain). Conditioned = through pairing (tokens, praise / "no"). Generalized = paired with many backups, works regardless of any one MO (money, tokens). Applies to reinforcers and punishers.
  • Automatic (behavior produces its own consequence) vs. socially mediated (someone delivers it).
  • Escape = Ends a present aversive · Avoidance = Averts one that hasn't occurred (both negative reinforcement).
  • Premack: a high-probability behavior reinforces a low-probability one ("first work, then play"). Osborne (1969) is the classroom demo.
  • Matching law (Herrnstein): relative response rate matches relative reinforcement rate — a straight line, slope ≈ 1.0. Neef (1994): rate, quality, delay, and effort each pull choice.
Three harmful side effects of punishment (Sidman 1993): 1) emotional / aggressive reactions; 2) escape and avoidance of the punisher, the person delivering it, and the setting; 3) suppression without new learning — it doesn't teach a replacement, so the behavior often returns. (Also acceptable: modeling of coercion; the punishing agent is negatively reinforced.)

Part 6 · Science & Foundations Refresher

  • GET A CAB — the 7 dimensions (Baer, Wolf & Risley, 1968). Generality is comprised of: change that lasts over time, appears in other settings, and spreads to other behaviors.
  • Dr. PEEP² — the 6 attitudes of science. Determinism vs. free will = opposites; determinism is required for a science of behavior.
  • IV = what you manipulate (the intervention; "I change it"). DV = what you measure (the behavior; it depends on the IV).
  • Respondent behavior = elicited by an antecedent stimulus (reflexive, S-R; Pavlov/Watson).
  • Three levels of scientific understanding: Description → Prediction (correlation) → Control (functional relation).

Application Answer Key (the written section — work these out loud)

  1. Describe an abolishing operation + example. An MO that decreases the current value of a reinforcer and abates related behavior. Ex: right after a big meal, food is a weak reinforcer, so working for a snack drops (satiation).
  2. Define an establishing operation + example. An MO that increases the current value of a reinforcer and evokes related behavior. Ex: water deprivation → water is a strong reinforcer, so getting-a-drink behavior increases.
  3. Reinforcement / punishment 2×2. Add+increase = positive reinforcement; remove+increase = negative reinforcement; add+decrease = positive punishment; remove+decrease = negative punishment. (Positive/negative = add/remove, not good/bad.)
  4. Conditioned reinforcer + two examples. A stimulus that gained its reinforcing power through a history of pairing with existing reinforcers. Ex: money; a token or sticker (also praise, grades).
  5. SD vs. SΔ. Given an SD, the response has been reinforced (reinforcement available) → more likely. Given an SΔ, it has not (unavailable) → less likely.
  6. Three harmful side effects of punishment. 1) emotional/aggressive reactions; 2) escape and avoidance of the punisher, the person, and the setting; 3) suppression without teaching a replacement (the behavior returns).
  7. Label the three-term contingency + example. SD → R → SR+ (antecedent → behavior → reinforcing consequence). Ex: teacher asks "What's 2+2?" (SD) → child says "4" (R) → "Great job!" (SR+).
  8. Define concept formation. A repertoire requiring generalization WITHIN a stimulus class (respond to all examples) AND discrimination BETWEEN classes (not to non-examples). Both are necessary.
  9. Name the three levels of scientific understanding. 1) Description (observable, quantifiable facts), 2) Prediction (correlation), 3) Control (a functional relation — the highest level).
  10. Two schedules + a key word each. Fixed Ratio (FR) — reinforcement after a set number of responses; key words "every Nth / after each X responses." Variable Interval (VI) — first response after a varying time; key words "on average every X minutes / unpredictable time."
  11. Which dimension of behavior can be shaped? → All of them (frequency, latency, duration, intensity).
  12. Generality is comprised of: lasting over time, transfer across settings, and spread to other behaviors.
  13. The M&M bag the child can't open → which CMO? Transitive (CMO-T) — the sealed bag establishes "help/opening" as a reinforcer and evokes the mand.
  14. The yellow cardstock has become what kind of stimulus? A reflexive CMO (CMO-R) — it warns of a worsening situation, raising the value of escape and evoking the tantrum.
  15. The critical test for stimulus equivalence. Emergence of untrained relations — the combined symmetry-and-transitivity test (train A→B and B→C, then test C→A), reflexivity assumed.
  16. A deadline for meeting a schedule's response requirement = limited hold.
  17. Gradual change of an antecedent stimulus so the response occurs to a new / partly-changed stimulus = fading.
